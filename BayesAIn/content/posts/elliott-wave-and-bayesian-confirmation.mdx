Elliott Wave analysis has a credibility problem in quantitative circles. Critics point to its subjectivity — two analysts looking at the same chart can count waves differently and arrive at opposite predictions. This criticism is fair as far as it goes, but it misses something.

The problem is not that Elliott Wave is wrong. The problem is that practitioners often use it as a prediction engine when it is better used as a hypothesis generator.

Treated as a hypothesis — "the market is currently in wave 3 of an impulse, which means the next likely move is continuation to a target around X" — Elliott Wave becomes amenable to Bayesian updating. You can assign a prior probability to the hypothesis, observe subsequent price action, and compute how much that action either confirms or disconfirms the wave count.

When a pattern that looks like wave 3 continuation is also accompanied by momentum expansion, above-average volume, and narrowing breadth divergence, your posterior probability of the hypothesis rises meaningfully. When those confirming signals are absent, it falls. The wave count is now one input into a belief state, not the final word.

This synthesis is at the heart of the methodology behind BayesAIn Strategies. Elliott Wave provides the hypothesis space; Bayesian reasoning provides the machinery for evaluating and updating the hypotheses against evidence.
