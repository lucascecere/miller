Expected value is taught as the foundation of rational decision-making under uncertainty. Multiply each possible outcome by its probability, sum the results, and choose the option with the highest number. Simple. Elegant. Dangerously incomplete.

The problem is that expected value treats probabilities as fixed. In most real-world decision environments — and especially in financial markets — probabilities are not fixed. They are estimates that should update as evidence accumulates. A framework that ignores this is not rational; it is merely mechanical.

Bayesian decision theory extends expected value by coupling it to a belief-updating mechanism. Your probabilities are not constants but distributions over hypotheses. As you observe outcomes, you revise those distributions. The decision you make at time T is not the decision you should make at time T+1 if the evidence has shifted.

This matters practically. A trading strategy that performed well under one market regime may perform poorly under another — not because the underlying logic was wrong, but because the implicit probability assignments embedded in the strategy stopped being accurate. Bayesian practitioners notice this shift earlier, because they are explicitly modeling the evolution of beliefs.

The deeper point is that uncertainty is not a bug to be minimized. It is the medium in which decisions are made. The goal is not to eliminate uncertainty but to reason inside it correctly.
