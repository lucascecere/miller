The hardest part of teaching probabilistic thinking is not the mathematics. The mathematics, for finance students who have already survived calculus and statistics, is manageable. The hard part is the intuitions.

Most students arrive with deeply embedded deterministic mental models. They want to know what will happen, not what the probability distribution over what might happen looks like. When you ask them to think about the distribution of future returns rather than the single most likely return, something uncomfortable happens. They feel like they are giving up on knowing something.

The reframe I have found most effective is to point out that the deterministic intuition is an illusion. The future return is already a random variable. The question is whether you model it as one or pretend it is not. Pretending it is not a random variable does not make it one — it just means your mental model is worse than the reality you are navigating.

Once students accept that uncertainty is the terrain, probabilistic tools start to feel like navigation aids rather than admissions of ignorance. That shift — from uncertainty as problem to uncertainty as the thing you are trying to map — is the core of what I am trying to teach.

After thirty years, I am still discovering new ways to make that shift click for different kinds of learners.
