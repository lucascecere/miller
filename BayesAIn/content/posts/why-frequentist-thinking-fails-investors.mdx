The dominant statistical paradigm in academic finance is frequentist. Studies are designed around null hypotheses, p-values are the measure of evidence, and results are reported as confidence intervals. This framework has produced a large and influential body of research.

It has also produced a replication crisis, a proliferation of factor strategies that work in backtests and fail in production, and a systematic underestimation of tail risk.

The core issue is that frequentist methods assume the existence of a stable, repeatable data-generating process. The 95% confidence interval means something precise: if you ran this experiment infinitely many times, 95% of the intervals would contain the true parameter. That interpretation only makes sense if the experiment is, in principle, repeatable under the same conditions.

Financial markets are not like that. The data-generating process changes — due to regulatory shifts, technological innovation, changes in market microstructure, and the behavior of participants who are themselves updating their models based on published research. The frequentist framework, applied naively, treats market data as if it were a stable physical process. It is not.

Bayesian methods do not require this assumption. A Bayesian analysis is explicit about the model it is using and about the prior beliefs it is updating. When the market regime shifts, the practitioner updates the model — not as a special case, but as the normal course of operation. That is closer to how thoughtful investors actually behave, and it is a more honest framework for the task.
