Every market participant enters a trade with a prior — a belief about where the price is going before looking at any data. Most traders never make this prior explicit, which means they cannot update it systematically as new information arrives.

The Bayesian framework forces that explicitness. You state your prior probability distribution over outcomes, observe new evidence, and compute a posterior. The posterior becomes your next prior. This cycle of updating is the core of how Bayesian decision-making differs from classical statistical approaches.

In financial markets, this has direct implications for signal interpretation. A momentum signal that looks strong under frequentist analysis may be much weaker when your prior — based on sector conditions, macro regime, and historical base rates — is properly accounted for. Bayesian thinking forces you to ask: how much does this signal actually move the needle on my prior?

The challenge is calibration. A well-calibrated prior is derived from relevant historical data, adjusted for structural shifts in the market environment. Getting that calibration right is, in many ways, the entire art of quantitative finance.
